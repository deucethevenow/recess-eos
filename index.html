1 The Big Picture

EOS gives us a shared language and rhythm for running the company.

The Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS) strengthens six components of our business: Vision, People, Data, Issues, Process, and Traction. When all six are healthy, we move fast and stay aligned.

The most visible parts of EOS for everyone on the team are:

Every 90 days
🏔
Rocks
Our top 3-7 priorities for the quarter. Clear outcomes, one owner each.
Every week
📋
L10 Meeting
Structured 90-min meeting. Same agenda every week. Scorecard, Rocks, Issues.
Every week
📊
Scorecard
5-15 numbers that tell us if the business is healthy. Reviewed weekly.
Ongoing
✅
To-Dos
7-day action items. Created in meetings, due next week. Target: 90%+ done.

2 What Are Rocks?

The 3-7 most important things we must accomplish this quarter.

The name comes from a metaphor: imagine a jar. If you put in sand (small tasks) first, there's no room for big rocks (priorities). But if you place the big rocks first, the sand fills in around them.

Rocks are not a project list. They're not everything we're working on. They're the vital few things that, if we accomplished nothing else this quarter, would make it a great quarter.

💡
A good Rock has three things:
1. One owner — a single person accountable (not a team)
2. A measurable outcome — what "done" looks like, specifically
3. A due date — always the last day of the quarter

Examples of Good vs. Bad Rocks

✅
Good Rock
"Launch self-serve onboarding with 50 activated accounts by June 30"
Specific. Measurable. Clear "done."
❌
Bad Rock
"Improve the onboarding experience"
Vague. When is it done? How do you know?

3 The Rock Cascade

Company Rocks flow down to departments, then to individuals. Alignment from top to bottom.

Rocks cascade downward. Leadership sets company priorities first. Then each department asks: "What do we need to deliver to make this happen?" Then individuals do the same.

Company Rock
"Launch self-serve onboarding for mid-market"
Owner: Deuce • Due: June 30 • Set by leadership team
Department Rock
"Build onboarding flow v1 with 3 key integrations"
Owner: Eng Lead • Due: June 15 • Supports company Rock above
Individual Rock
"Ship onboarding UI with self-serve sign-up flow"
Owner: Frontend Engineer • Due: May 15
Department Rock
"Generate 50 qualified self-serve leads"
Owner: Danny • Due: June 20 • Supports company Rock above
Individual Rock
"Write and launch 12 nurture emails for self-serve segment"
Owner: Marketing • Due: May 30
👉
Not everyone needs a formal Rock. Leadership always has Rocks (3-7 each). Department heads may cascade to their teams. Individual contributors often execute through weekly To-Dos from L10 meetings rather than formal Rocks.

4 How We Track Rocks

Rocks are tracked at the owner's level. Weekly status is binary: on-track or off-track.

Every week in the L10 meeting, each Rock owner gives a one-word update: on-track or off-track. No stories, no percentages. The whole Rock review takes 5 minutes.

Rock Owner Status Milestones
Launch self-serve onboarding Deuce On Track 2 of 3 complete
Hit $3M pipeline by Q2 end Danny Off Track 1 of 4 complete
Launch supplier NPS program Ashton On Track 3 of 3 complete
Reduce AR to <40 days Claire On Track 2 of 3 complete
⚠️
Off-track is not bad — hiding it is. When a Rock goes off-track, it drops to the Issues section of the L10 meeting. The team discusses what's blocking it and creates To-Dos to get it back on track. The system only works if people are honest about status.

Where Each Level Gets Tracked

Level Who Reports How Often Where
Company Rock owner (leadership) Weekly Leadership L10, Section 3
Department Dept head Weekly Department L10
Individual The person Weekly / biweekly 1-on-1 or dept meeting

5 Scoring: Done or Not Done

At the end of the quarter, every Rock is scored. No partial credit.

EOS scoring is binary. The measurable outcome was either achieved or it wasn't. An 80% finished Rock is scored Not Done. This feels harsh at first, but it forces us to set realistic Rocks and finish what we start.

✔️

✔️

🎯 Quarterly Scorecard: 3/4 = 75%
Target: 80%+ completion rate. Below 80% means either the Rocks were too ambitious or there were execution issues. Either way, it becomes a discussion topic for next quarter's planning. An unfinished Rock can become a new Rock next quarter — it doesn't disappear, it gets re-prioritized.

The L10 Meeting (90 min)

The Level 10 meeting is the weekly heartbeat. Same agenda, every week, no exceptions.

Time Section What Happens
5 min Segue Share personal or professional good news
5 min Scorecard Review weekly numbers. Only discuss off-track metrics.
5 min Rock Review Each owner: on-track or off-track. No discussion yet.
5 min Headlines Customer and employee news. Good or bad.
5 min To-Do Review Done or not done. Target 90%+. No excuses.
60 min IDS Identify the real issue, Discuss root cause, Solve it. This is where the real work happens.
5 min Conclude Recap new To-Dos, cascading messages, rate the meeting 1-10.

7 The Key Rules

These keep the system honest.

  • ✅
    One owner per Rock. If two people "co-own" it, nobody owns it. One name. That person can delegate work, but they're accountable for the outcome.
  • ✅
    3-7 Rocks per person, max. If you have 12 "Rocks," those are projects. Cut to the vital few. What really must happen?
  • ✅
    Binary scoring only. Done or Not Done. No partial credit, no percentages. This forces realistic goal-setting.
  • ✅
    Rocks must align upward. Every department Rock should connect to a company Rock. If it doesn't, ask: is this really a priority?
  • 🚫
    Don't solve off-track Rocks in Rock Review. Just say "off-track" and move on. It goes to the IDS section where the team has 60 minutes to actually solve problems.
  • 🚫
    Don't change Rocks mid-quarter. Rocks are 90-day commitments. If something truly changes, it gets formally dropped and discussed — not quietly replaced.
  • 🚫
    Don't skip the meeting. The L10 happens every week. Same day, same time. Consistency creates the rhythm. Skipping "just this once" breaks the habit.
💪
The 80% target is aspirational, not punitive. Scoring below 80% isn't failure — it's data. It tells us we're either overcommitting or under-executing. Both are fixable. The goal is to get better at setting the right Rocks each quarter.
